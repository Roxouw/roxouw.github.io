# Web Conversion Patterns

This file condenses durable patterns from UX and conversion research into actionable guidance for sales pages and service websites.

Use it when the task involves:

- improving hero sections
- rewriting CTA copy
- restructuring offer pages
- reducing text overload
- increasing readability
- improving proof sections
- refining mobile-first landing pages

## Durable rules

## 1. Above-the-fold clarity wins

- Users should understand what is being offered, for whom, and what to do next within a few seconds.
- The first screen should not depend on exploration to make sense.
- The primary CTA should be visible early and use specific action language.

Implication for Rosso:

- Hero sections should state the business outcome, not just the service category.
- One primary CTA and one secondary CTA are usually enough.

## 2. Pages are scanned, not read linearly

- Users skim headings, emphasized phrases, bullets, and short paragraphs.
- Dense text blocks reduce comprehension and increase abandonment.
- Good formatting improves both comprehension and confidence.

Implication for Rosso:

- Prefer 1 to 3 short paragraphs or bullet clusters over long explanation blocks.
- Use headings that communicate value, not vague themes.

## 3. Text width and text volume affect conversion

- Long line lengths reduce readability.
- Body copy generally reads best in moderate measure rather than full-width blocks.
- Too much text feels expensive to read.

Practical heuristic:

- Keep body copy around moderate line length and avoid very wide paragraph blocks.
- Break sections when a paragraph starts explaining too many ideas at once.

## 4. CTA performance comes from clarity, not hype

- CTA tests often succeed when copy is more explicit about the outcome.
- CTA buttons should set expectations.
- Users respond better when they know what happens next.

Good CTA patterns:

- Pedir orçamento
- Ver opções e preços
- Falar no WhatsApp
- Quero um site como esse

Weak CTA patterns:

- Saiba mais
- Conheça
- Explorar
- Descobrir

## 5. Proof should reduce uncertainty, not decorate the page

- Real examples, specific outcomes, and context increase confidence.
- Visual proof works better when paired with a clear explanation of why it matters.
- “Real use” imagery or real project previews can increase trust when they help evaluation.

Implication for Rosso:

- Keep project previews tied to outcome: authority, more inquiries, easier qualification, stronger first impression.
- Case tags should explain commercial value, not only aesthetic choices.

## 6. Mobile needs compression, not just stacking

- Small screens increase fatigue from long copy and repeated blocks.
- Important actions should appear before the user feels they are entering a long wall of content.
- Text in images or previews must remain legible.

Implication for Rosso:

- On mobile, compress hero, trim checklist lines, shorten proof chips, and keep CTAs easy to tap.
- If a section becomes long, split it into simpler sub-blocks.

## 7. Forms and friction need expectation-setting

- Shorter forms usually reduce friction, but relevance matters more than arbitrary minimalism.
- CTA and surrounding copy should explain what users receive after submitting or clicking.

Implication for Rosso:

- Contact sections should say what happens next: fast response, clear proposal, direct contact, no bureaucracy.

## 8. Layout order matters

Reliable order for service and landing pages:

1. clear promise
2. visible CTA
3. fast trust/proof
4. explanation of problem or missed opportunity
5. how the service solves it
6. examples or delivered projects
7. pricing or offer framing
8. FAQ / objection handling
9. final CTA

Use this order as a baseline, not a prison.

## Practical heuristics by category

## Headline

- Lead with the business result when possible.
- Avoid vague words like “inovador”, “excelência”, “solução completa”.
- If the page is niche-specific, mention the niche early.

## Subheadline

- Explain the offer in one compact sentence.
- Clarify outcome, mechanism, or scope.
- Avoid repeating the headline with synonyms.

## Proof chips / mini-badges

- Use short, specific claims.
- Prefer “WhatsApp em destaque” over abstract labels.
- Keep count low enough to scan quickly.

## Case-study text

- Answer: who is this for, what changed, why that matters.
- Mention commercial effect before design flavor.

## Pricing blocks

- Distinguish plans by job-to-be-done, not by vague prestige language alone.
- Keep benefit bullets short and concrete.
- Reduce decorative labels if they don’t help decisions.

## FAQ

- Handle objections that delay contact: timing, price expectation, what is included, how the process works.
- Keep answers direct and action-oriented.

## Copy density heuristics

- Hero description: 1 short paragraph
- Proof chips: 3 to 6 max
- Benefit cards: 1 short paragraph + compact tags
- Case description: 1 short paragraph
- FAQ answers: 2 to 4 sentences
- Final CTA support text: 1 compact sentence

If a section needs more than that, split the content instead of enlarging the block.

## Red flags

- decorative headings that do not explain value
- long paragraphs with multiple ideas
- CTA labels that hide the next step
- proof that talks only about design taste
- tiny text over images
- wide paragraph blocks on desktop
- mobile sections that only “stack” without reducing content

## How to use this reference

For each requested page change:

1. pick the relevant categories
2. browse the web if freshness or precision matters
3. extract 3 to 5 applicable findings
4. turn them into concrete edits for Rosso
5. keep only what improves clarity, trust, or contact rate
